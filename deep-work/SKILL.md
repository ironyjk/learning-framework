# Deep Work

> Background and theory: Read references/foundation.md



## Layer Position

**L2 (Environment · Time)** — Same layer as pomodoro-and-focus. The *container* in which all learning from L3–L6 occurs. When L2 is broken, upper layers are meaningless. For beginners or those in states of attentional collapse, train with pomodoro first, then move on to deep work.

## Practical Rules (Newport's Proposals)

### 1. Schedule Blocks
- Pre-plan the day in blocks. Each block = 1–4 hours.
- Deep work blocks have *no meetings, no notifications*.

### 2. Quantify
- Record weekly deep work hours. Set a target (e.g., 15 hours/week).
- Track *total depth*, not merely time.

### 3. Communication Rules
- Limit email checks to 2–3 times per day.
- Minimize real-time messengers — "always online" makes shallow the default.
- Set predictable response times.

### 4. Allow Boredom
- One cause of attention fragmentation is *reduced boredom tolerance*. The habit of pulling out the phone while waiting in line or in an elevator erodes the capacity for deep work.
- Deliberately practice "waiting without a phone."

### 5. Reduce Social Media
- Use only the minimum needed for asset creation. Newport himself uses no personal SNS.
- Context varies. The goal is not complete exclusion but *conscious use*.

### 6. Cap Shallow Time
- Shallow work is necessary, but set a *ceiling* to protect deep work.

## Korean Context Examples

**Corporate developer (Rhythmic)**:
- 9:30–11:30 AM = deep block. Slack off, focus solely on implementing one feature.
- 11:30–12:00 = batch reply to messengers and email.
- 1:30–3:00 PM = another deep block (code review · design).
- Rest of the afternoon = meetings · PR reviews · pairing.
- Secures 3–4 hours of deep work per day.

**Graduate student (Bimodal)**:
- 4 hours each weekday morning = deep (papers · analysis).
- Every Wednesday = "open day" (advising · meetings · seminars).
- 2 weeks before a paper deadline = Monastic mode, cancel all external commitments.

**Marketer (Journalistic)**:
- Meeting-heavy role. When a 1-hour gap opens, immediately plunge into high-difficulty work.
- Only viable for the trained. Beginners should start with Rhythmic.

### Friction with Korean Organizational and Learning Culture

Challenges and responses for implementing deep work in Korean workplace and learning environments:

| Environmental Condition | Friction | Realistic Response |
|---|---|---|
| **Always-on messenger · KakaoTalk work** | Expectation of immediate reply | Team agreement on "2 hours of morning focus, then batch reply." If impossible, use only the quiet period early morning or just after lunch. |
| **Excessive meetings** | Fragmentation | Lock "no-meeting blocks" on personal calendar on Wednesday and Friday. If non-negotiable, use time before/after commute. |
| **Test-prep reading rooms · SKY culture** | Boasting about hours | Track total deep-work time. Sitting for 8 hours may yield only 2 hours of deep work. Track quality. |
| **Binge-watching online lectures · finishing EBS** | Passive consumption ≠ deep work | Lectures are shallow (information intake). Deep work happens in retrieval · problem-solving · self-explanation blocks. |
| **Self-study late-night sessions · early rising** | Sleep deprivation causes cognitive damage | 7 hours of sleep > early rising. Match to your personal chronotype. |
| **Family · caregiving burden** | Hard to secure long blocks | Bimodal (weekend concentration) or accumulate 15–25 minute pomodoros. Consistency matters more than block length. |
| **Open-plan office seating** | Weak environmental control | Noise-canceling headphones, booking meeting rooms, using remote work. Environmental design is stronger than willpower. |

## Anti-patterns

- **"Always focused"** — Misunderstanding deep work as a constantly sustainable mode. In reality, ~4 hours/day is the ceiling (even for top-tier knowledge workers, Newport included).
- **"Just focus" by willpower without blocks** — Trying to overcome by willpower without environmental design. Repeated failure.
- **Goal of email zero** — Eliminating shallow is not the goal. *Proportion* is what's adjusted.
- **Attempting perfect monastic** — Most fail. Start with Rhythmic.
- **Silencing notifications while keeping the phone nearby** — Mere presence drags attention (Ward et al. 2017).
- **Using "Slow Productivity" to dodge accountability** — When the issue is structural overload, only changing personal focus methods won't solve it.

## Limitations

1. **Assumes personal autonomy** — Limited applicability for roles without control over one's own schedule (customer-facing, medical on-call).
2. **Evidence level of the research** — Newport's books are essay- and case-driven. Theoretical synthesis, not controlled experiments.
3. **Cultural differences** — Friction for individual practice in Korean organizational culture (always-connected, expectation of immediate messenger replies).
4. **Ignores emotional labor and caregiving** — "Protecting deep work" in the context of relationships and family can become selfish. Balance is needed.
5. **Burnout risk** — The sustainability of 4 hours of high-intensity deep work every day is questionable. Recovery, shallow work, and rest are also needed.
6. **Cult-like application** — The phenomenon where "filling deep time" becomes an end in itself.

## What to Use Alongside This Framework

- **pomodoro-and-focus** — The *implementation tool* of Deep Work. Construct deep blocks in pomodoro units.
- **deliberate-practice** — The qualitative standard of deep work. Not merely focusing, but aiming *just beyond the edge* of capability.
- **metalearning** — Self-analysis of what constitutes your deep work versus your shallow work.
- **zettelkasten / second-brain-para** — Capturing and organizing the outputs of deep work.

## When This Framework Is *Wrong*

- Can't sustain focus for even 5 minutes → train `pomodoro-and-focus` first
- Learning itself is insufficient → `active-recall` / `spaced-repetition`
- Skill improvement → `deliberate-practice`
- Information organization problem → `second-brain-para`
